New Puppy

The First-Year Puppy Checklist Every New Owner Needs

By The Scattered Nest

← Back to all posts

When we first brought Scotty home, I thought I was ready. I had the crate. I had the food. I had approximately 400 browser tabs open about puppy training.

What I did not have was any kind of system for remembering when his next round of shots was due, which dewormer he'd already had, or what the vet said about that weird spot on his belly. Within two weeks I was Googling things I'd already been told and hoping I hadn't missed anything important.

If you're about to bring home a puppy (or recently did and you're reading this at 2am while they chew on something they shouldn't be), this checklist is for you.

The First Week: Survival Mode

Nobody's organized in week one. That's okay. Just focus on three things:

Get that first vet visit booked. Most vets want to see new puppies within a few days of coming home, especially if they haven't started their vaccine series yet. Write down the appointment date somewhere you'll actually see it.

Start a simple health log. It doesn't have to be fancy. A notebook, a notes app, even a piece of paper stuck to the fridge. Write down what they eat, how much, and what's coming out the other end. Your vet will ask about all of this at that first visit.

Save the breeder or rescue paperwork. Whatever documents came with your puppy (vaccine records, microchip info, health guarantees), put them in one place right now. Not "later." Now. Future you will be frantically searching for these at some point.

Weeks 2 through 8: Building the Foundation

This is when things start to feel more real and more overwhelming. Puppies need a lot of vet visits in their first few months, and it's easy to lose track.

Vaccination schedule. Puppies typically need a series of shots spaced 3 to 4 weeks apart, usually starting around 6 to 8 weeks old. Your vet will give you the schedule, but you need to actually remember the dates. Write them down. Set phone reminders. Tattoo them on your arm if you have to.

Deworming. Most puppies need multiple rounds of deworming in the first few months. Your vet will tell you when, but again, this is the kind of thing that slips through the cracks when you're also dealing with potty training at 3am.

Weight tracking. Puppies grow fast, and your vet uses weight to calculate medication doses and track development. Weigh your puppy regularly (the bathroom scale trick works great for small breeds) and jot it down.

Behaviour notes. Start paying attention to what's normal for your puppy. How do they react to new people? Loud sounds? Other dogs? These observations become really valuable later, whether you're working with a trainer or just trying to understand your dog better.

Months 2 through 6: The Busy Season

This is when you're juggling the most. Vaccine boosters, spay or neuter discussions, teething, socialisation windows, and probably some furniture damage.

Keep tracking vet visits. Write down what happened at each appointment, what was given, and what's due next. It sounds tedious, but your vet's office sees hundreds of puppies. Your notes are your backup.

Start a medication and supplement log. If your puppy is on flea and tick prevention, heartworm meds, or any supplements, log the dates and doses. This is especially important if your household has more than one person handling pet care.

Note any food changes. Puppies often transition foods during this period. Write down what you switched from, what you switched to, and how their stomach handled it. If something doesn't agree with them, you'll want to remember what it was.

Months 6 through 12: Settling In

The chaos starts to calm down (a little). Your puppy is probably through their main vaccine series and starting to settle into a routine.

Annual vaccine and checkup reminders. Set these now before you forget. A calendar reminder a month before things are due gives you time to book without scrambling.

Ongoing tracking. By now, you know your dog's normal. Keep logging anything that changes, eating, bathroom, energy levels, behaviour. This is exactly the kind of data that helps your vet catch issues early (and it's why I wrote that whole other blog post about it).

Training progress. If you're working on obedience, reactivity, or any kind of behaviour goals, keep notes. What worked, what didn't, what you want to try next. This stuff is easy to forget, and having a record helps you see how far you've come (even on the days it doesn't feel like it).

The Honest Truth

Nobody does all of this perfectly. I definitely didn't. But having even a basic system, something to scribble in when you remember, makes the whole first year feel less like you're winging it.
