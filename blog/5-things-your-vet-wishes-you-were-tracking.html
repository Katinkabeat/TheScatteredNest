5 Things Your Vet Wishes You Were Tracking

By The Scattered Nest

← Back to all posts

When Zero had his last vet visit, the tech asked me when his symptoms first started. I stared at her like she'd asked me to recite pi to the 50th digit.

I knew it was "a while ago" but was it two weeks? A month? Somewhere between that Tuesday it rained and the day Scotty ate a sock? No clue.

That was the moment I started writing things down. Not because I became an organized person overnight (ha), but because I realized my vet was trying to help my dogs and I was making it harder by relying on my very scattered memory.

Here are five things your vet actually wants you to keep track of between visits. None of them are complicated, and all of them can make a real difference at your next appointment.

1. Changes in Eating and Drinking Habits

You know your dog's normal. Maybe they inhale breakfast in 3 seconds flat or they're a grazer who takes their sweet time. Either way, when that pattern shifts, eating less, drinking more, suddenly turning their nose up at food they normally love, that's information your vet needs.

The tricky part is that these changes often happen gradually. By the time you notice, you can't pinpoint when it started. A simple daily log (even just a checkmark for "normal" or a note when something seems off) gives your vet a timeline they can actually work with.

2. Bathroom Habits (Yes, Really)

Nobody wants to talk about poop at the dinner table, but your vet genuinely wants to know. Changes in frequency, consistency, colour, or straining can be early signs of everything from dietary issues to something more serious.

You don't need to write a novel, just a quick note when something looks different from your dog's usual. "Soft stool two days in a row" is the kind of detail that helps your vet connect dots you might not see on your own.

3. Behavioural Changes

This one is close to my heart. With two reactive dogs, I track behaviours and triggers constantly, not just for training, but because changes in behaviour can signal pain, anxiety, or health issues that aren't obvious.

Is your dog suddenly hiding more? Growling when they're normally chill? Pacing at night? Reluctant to jump on the couch they usually claim as their throne? These are things your vet wants to hear about, and "it started about three weeks ago" is infinitely more helpful than "I dunno, recently?"

4. Medication and Supplement Schedules

If your dog takes any medications, flea and tick prevention, joint supplements, or even probiotics, your vet needs to know what they're getting and when. This is especially important if you see multiple specialists or if anyone in the household shares feeding duties.

It sounds simple, but when you're sitting in the exam room trying to remember which supplement you switched last month and whether you gave this morning's dose, things get fuzzy fast. A written log keeps everyone (including future-you) on the same page.

5. Weight and Body Condition

Weight changes in dogs can be subtle, especially if you see them every day. A pound or two might not seem like much, but on a small dog like Scotty, that can be a significant percentage of his body weight.

You don't need a scale for every weigh-in (though bathroom scales work, step on, pick up the dog, do the math). Even noting things like "ribs feel more prominent" or "belly looks rounder" gives your vet useful data points between annual visits.

The Good News: It Doesn't Have to Be Complicated

You don't need a medical degree or a colour-coded spreadsheet. A simple tracker, even just a page you glance at each day, is enough to give your vet the kind of information that leads to better care and earlier catches.
